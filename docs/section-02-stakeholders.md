# Section 2: Stakeholder & Permissions Map

*Project Site: Port Cygnet Boardwalk, Lymington Road, Cygnet, Tasmania — 60m gallery section subject to final site audit*

---

## Overview

The Palimpsest Path operates on public infrastructure over a sensitive waterway, requiring proactive stakeholder engagement before any chalk is applied. The project's low-impact materials are a significant asset: calcium carbonate chalk is water-soluble, pH-neutral, and biodegradable. A clear permissions trail nonetheless protects both the project and its lead.

The project has a strong local foundation: an existing acquaintance with Councillor Paul Gibson at Huon Valley Council provides a warm entry point for the permit process, and the sole neighbouring resident to the Lymington Road site is known to the project lead. Both relationships should be activated early in Phase 0.

---

## Permissions Pathway

Complete the following steps during Phase 0, in sequence. Do not install signage or chalk stations until written Council approval is in hand.

1. **Contact Councillor Paul Gibson** — First step, before any formal submission. Brief the project verbally, gauge any known sensitivities about the Lymington Road site, and ask for the correct internal contact for ephemeral public art permits.

2. **Identify boardwalk infrastructure owner** — Confirm whether the Lymington Road boardwalk is owned and managed by Huon Valley Council, Crown Land Tasmania, or another body. This determines who issues the primary permit. Councillor Gibson is the fastest route to this answer.

3. **Submit formal permit application** — Address to the correct department (likely Community Development or Infrastructure). Include: project brief, calcium carbonate material safety data, site map of the nominated 60m section, FXA301 academic context, and evaluation methodology summary.

4. **UTAS / FXA301 supervisor sign-off** — Confirm whether the Raspberry Pi Pico W sensor methodology and QR photo archive require formal UTAS Human Research Ethics Committee (HREC) review, or whether FXA301 supervisor sign-off is sufficient. Raise this early — a full HREC review can take 4–6 weeks.

5. **Consult Lymington Road neighbour** — In-person conversation during Phase 0. Explain the project, its duration, and what the chalk stations look like. Note her response in the project log. No formal approval required.

6. **Brief Huon Valley Arts** — Introduce the project by email or phone. Invite them to co-promote, share the sidewalkcircus.org gallery link with their networks, or contribute prompts if interested. Do not position as formal partners without their agreement.

## Key Permissions Risks

**Permit delayed or refused [HIGH]** — Engage Councillor Gibson early to warm the pathway. Have a scaled-back fallback proposal ready (e.g. walkway zone only, no bannister stations) if the full scope raises concerns.

**Boardwalk owned by non-Council body [MEDIUM]** — Identify ownership in the first Phase 0 week. Do not assume Council ownership. Adjust the permit application target accordingly.

**UTAS HREC review required [MEDIUM]** — A 4–6 week HREC timeline could push Week 1 back significantly. Raise with FXA301 supervisor as a first-week Phase 0 priority.

**Neighbour concern about visual amenity [LOW]** — The existing relationship with the project lead is a strong mitigant. An early, relaxed in-person conversation is far preferable to any formal process.

**Local arts orgs not engaged [LOW]** — Their involvement is valuable but not critical to the permit pathway. Brief them in parallel with other Phase 0 activities rather than sequentially.
