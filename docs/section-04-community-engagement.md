# Section 4: Community Engagement & Participation Design

*How the Palimpsest Path feels from the inside — and how people find their way in.*

---

## 4.1 Design Philosophy: The Lowest Possible Door

The central participation challenge of the Palimpsest Path is not awareness — it is permission. Regular boardwalk users already know the space. What they lack is an explicit invitation to treat it as somewhere they are allowed to leave a mark. Every design decision in this section is oriented toward lowering that threshold: making the first act of participation feel small, safe, and reversible.

This is why the project begins with dance steps rather than prompts. A traced shoe outline makes no claim on anyone's identity or opinion. It is inherently playful, physically grounded, and invites a bodily rather than intellectual response. Colour and words arrive only after the physical vocabulary is established.

The ephemeral medium reinforces this throughout. Chalk washes away. There is no permanent record of a hesitant first mark, no risk of public misjudgement outlasting the moment. This is the Palimpsest Effect working at its most practical: the knowledge that rain will come is a permission structure, not a limitation.

---

## 4.2 The Participant Journey

A walker encountering the Palimpsest Path for the first time moves through the following stages:

| Stage | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Discovery** | Walkers first encounter the dance step trail — chalk shoe outlines extending along the walkway surface. No signage explains them in Weeks 1–2. The absence of explanation is deliberate: curiosity precedes invitation. |
| **Orientation** | From Week 3, QR signage at the gallery entrance and exit provides context. The landing page at sidewalkcircus.org explains the project in plain language, shows photos of existing contributions, and gives clear participation instructions. |
| **First contact** | The chalk station is open. A laminated example card shows the tracing method and a sample flourish. The current week's prompt is written on a small chalkboard attached to the station. The threshold for a first mark is as low as adding a line to an existing step. |
| **Contribution** | The walker adds something — a traced step, a word, a drawing, a ribbon connecting two stories. This is the activation moment: the shift from observer to co-author. |
| **Return** | The dopamine reward of discovery — finding new contributions from others since the last visit — motivates return. The changing prompt each fortnight provides a new entry point for walkers who feel their first contribution was complete. |
| **Sharing** | Walkers who scan the QR code and find their contribution in the gallery may share the link with others, extending the project's reach beyond the boardwalk. |

## 4.3 The Chalk Station

The chalk station is the project's primary physical interface. Its design should communicate welcome, not instruction. A weather-resistant box mounted to the bannister, it contains coloured chalk, a laminated example card, and a small attached chalkboard displaying the current week's prompt. It should look like something that belongs on the boardwalk — not institutional, not precious.

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Contents** | Coloured calcium carbonate chalk: blues, greens, ochres reflecting the Cygnet waterway palette. White chalk for dance step tracing. Laminated example card showing traced shoe outline and a sample flourish. Small attached chalkboard displaying current week's prompt, updated by project lead. |
| **Location** | Primary station: mid-point of the 100m gallery section, maximum visibility. Additional stations deployed at Weeks 7–8 for peak intervention coverage. Exact bannister positions confirmed during Phase 0 site audit. |
| **Maintenance** | Check chalk stock daily during active weeks. Replenish as needed. Update prompt chalkboard at each fortnightly transition. Inspect mountings weekly for weather damage or vandalism. Photograph station condition as part of daily photo documentation. |
| **Weeks open** | Station visible but empty: Weeks 1–2. Station open with chalk: Weeks 3–8. |

## 4.4 Prompt Schedule & Rotation

Prompts are introduced fortnightly to allow sufficient time for responses to accumulate before the surface is refreshed. Each prompt is selected to progressively deepen engagement: from sensory observation, through personal identity, to social connection and collective vision. Suggested addition prompts — lower-stakes invitations attached to the step trail itself — run in parallel from Week 4.

| Week | Phase | Chalk Prompt | Engagement Channel |
|------|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **1–2** | Steps only | *No prompt — the steps are the invitation* | On-site discovery only |
| **3–4** | Opening invitation | *"Welcome to the Palimpsest Path. What did you notice on the water today?"* | QR signage live. Shopkeeper cards out. WordPress launch post. |
| **4–5** | Identity layer + suggested addition | *"I have walked this track for ___ years/months. My favourite spot is ___. Suggested: Add a flourish to any step you like."* | Brief regular walkers. Library display goes up. |
| **5–6** | Social ribbon + suggested addition | *"Draw a line from your story to someone else's. What do you have in common? Suggested: What music would these steps be danced to?"* | Mid-project WordPress post with gallery highlights. |
| **6–7** | Qualitative pulse | *"How connected to Cygnet do you feel today? [Isolated] ←———→ [Connected]"* | Daily Likert scale photography begins. |
| **7–8** | Collective vision | *"What do you wish for the future of this boardwalk?"* | Arts Council & Huon Valley Arts social media mention. |
| **8** | Grand Palimpsest | *"Fill the bannister with the faces of the people you imagine walk here."* | Community Walk day. Final QR gallery post. |

## 4.5 Engagement Network

The project's growth model is organic, radiating outward from an existing local network. Broad promotion is deliberately held back until Week 3, when the chalk station opens and there is something to invite people to. The silent discovery phase of Weeks 1–2 works best when walkers encounter the steps without prior expectation.

### Seed Network (Phase 0 — Week 2)

| Contact | Role |
|---------|------|
| **Regular walkers (known contacts)** | Brief two or three trusted regulars before Week 1 begins. Ask them to be unofficial "first responders" — people who will add chalk in the first days, seeding the space so it does not feel empty when strangers arrive. Do not ask them to promote the project; just to participate naturally. Their visible engagement in the early days is the most powerful invitation for other regulars watching. |
| **Jetty Rd neighbour** | Already consulted in Phase 0. If willing, she becomes the project's closest observer — someone who sees the boardwalk daily and can informally note changes in walker behaviour. |

### Activation Network (Week 3 onward)

| Contact | Role |
|---------|------|
| **Cygnet shopkeepers (known contacts)** | From Week 3: provide participating shops with a small stack of physical postcards carrying the QR code and a single line: "Something's happening on the boardwalk." Intrigue works better than explanation for this kind of project. Ask if they'd be willing to display the QR code in their window. No commitment required beyond that. |
| **Cygnet Community Arts Council** | Invite as a co-presenter in name if they are willing — this adds legitimacy for the Council permit conversation and gives them something low-effort to champion publicly. At minimum, ask for a mention in their newsletter or social media at launch (Week 3) and at the midpoint (Week 5). |
| **Cygnet Library** | From Week 4: ask the library to host a small rotating display of printed gallery screenshots. Update fortnightly with the best new contributions. The library gives the project a second physical home and reaches community members who may not walk the boardwalk regularly. |
| **Huon Valley Arts** | Brief by email or phone in Phase 0 (see Section 2). From Week 3, ask them to share the sidewalkcircus.org gallery link via their channels. A second mention at the Week 7 Grand Palimpsest peak is well-timed — the surface will be at its most visually rich. |
| **UTAS / FXA301** | Ask the FXA301 supervisor whether UTAS promotes community-engaged student projects through faculty newsletters or social media. A single mention at launch costs nothing and may reach unexpected audiences. |

## 4.6 Digital Engagement: sidewalkcircus.org

The WordPress site at sidewalkcircus.org serves two functions: a public-facing gallery that extends the project's reach beyond the boardwalk, and an archival record that supports the qualitative evaluation. It should be live and tested before the QR signage goes up in Phase 0.

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Site structure** | About: plain-language project description, participation instructions, privacy/consent statement. Weekly Archive: one post per week with 3–5 photos, a brief caption, and the current prompt. Gallery: full photo archive, browsable by week. Participate: QR code download, chalk station locations, current prompt. |
| **Posting rhythm** | Weekly archive post every Sunday — even just three photos and two sentences keeps the site alive and gives people a reason to check back. Special post after the first rain washout: before/after photo pair. Mid-project gallery highlights post at Week 5. Final post at Week 8: the full 8-week journey in a single scrollable gallery. |
| **QR workflow** | QR codes on boardwalk signage link directly to sidewalkcircus.org/participate. Walkers can photograph their own contributions and submit via a simple upload form or email link. All submissions reviewed before publication. No names published without consent. |
| **Sharing strategy** | Do not push broad social media promotion before Week 3. From Week 3: share weekly archive posts via Arts Council and Huon Valley Arts channels. The first rain washout post is the most naturally shareable content — time social sharing around it. Tag Huon Valley Council in the final Week 8 post as evidence of community impact. |

## 4.7 Participation Cautions

The following are known participation risks for community arts projects of this kind, and specific mitigations for the Palimpsest Path context:

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| **Over-promotion before Week 3** | Broad promotion before the chalk station opens creates expectation without experience. Walkers who arrive expecting an "art project" and find only some chalk steps may be disappointed. Hold the network activation until there is something to invite people into. |
| **Seeding too much yourself** | If the boardwalk surface is too densely covered by the project lead's own work, it can feel like someone else's territory rather than an open invitation. Leave visible space. Gaps are invitations. |
| **Explaining too much on signage** | Signage that over-explains the project's theoretical framework will deter casual participants. The QR page can carry depth; the physical signage should carry only: what to do, that it's welcome, and that it washes off. |
| **Prompt fatigue** | Too many simultaneous prompts can paralyse rather than invite. Keep the primary prompt to one per fortnight. The suggested addition prompt on the step trail is intentionally lower-stakes and does not compete with the main prompt. |
| **Removing community chalk** | After Week 3, do not remove chalk added by community members — even if aesthetically inconsistent with the project's visual style. Every mark is data and every mark is welcome. The palimpsest will sort it. |
