# Section 1: Rationale

*Neuroscience, Social Cohesion & the Therapeutic Landscape*

## 1.1 The Problem: Parallel Isolation in Shared Public Space

Despite the high volume of pedestrian traffic on the Cygnet boardwalk, many regular walkers experience a form of parallel isolation. While they share a physical space and may recognise familiar faces, the absence of a structured invitation to interact limits the development of meaningful social capital. Walkers occupy the same infrastructure yet remain, in Oldenburg's (1989) terms, without a "third place": an informal social environment distinct from home and work that facilitates spontaneous community connection.

This matters beyond the social. Research consistently identifies social isolation as a significant determinant of physical and cognitive health. Fancourt and Finn (2019) document the relationship between social disconnection and elevated cortisol levels, heightened inflammatory response, and increased risk of cognitive decline in aging populations. In the context of the Huon Valley, where an above-average proportion of residents are aged over 65 and geographic distance compounds isolation, the boardwalk represents both a symptom of this challenge and a genuine opportunity for low-cost, place-based intervention.

The Huon Valley Health and Wellbeing Strategy identifies Inclusion and Interaction as a priority action area, noting that built infrastructure which encourages incidental social contact can function as preventive health infrastructure. The Cygnet boardwalk — already embedded in daily routines — is precisely this kind of asset waiting to be activated.

## 1.2 The Intervention: Asynchronous Narrative Art

This project uses asynchronous narrative art — temporary chalk prompts, story fragments, and participatory dance step trails applied to the boardwalk's wooden bannisters and walkways — to create a community dialogue that transcends time-of-day barriers. A walker who passes at 7 am encounters the story left by someone at dusk the previous evening; the exchange is real, if displaced in time.

By inviting walkers to respond to collective prompts — "put a story to a face," complete a sentence, or follow a chalk dance step trail — the project transforms a transient thoroughfare into a site of layered social connection. Participation requires no artistic skill, no prior commitment, no financial outlay, and no synchronous attendance: the lowest possible barrier to entry.

## 1.3 Theoretical Foundations

### Social Glue Theory & Community Belonging

The project is grounded in Daykin's (2017) Social Glue theory, which posits that creative engagement in public spaces fosters a sense of belonging and community pride that exceeds the sum of individual interactions. Arts-based public interventions, Daykin argues, generate "social glue": the informal bonds and shared references that build community resilience over time. Critically, this effect does not require participants to meet; shared aesthetic experience in a common space is sufficient to generate a sense of collective identity.

Saarikallio's (2011) work on music and mood regulation extends this framework to the embodied dimension: creative engagement in public space activates identity construction and social agency, particularly among participants who do not self-identify as artists. The dance step trails component of this project is deliberately designed with this in mind — tracing shoe outlines and old-time dance instructions invites physical, playful participation that bypasses aesthetic self-consciousness.

### Neurological Mechanisms

The neurological basis for this intervention operates through two primary pathways:

**Dopamine reward pathway:** The discovery of a new chalk narrative — a story fragment, an answered prompt, a completed dance trail — constitutes a low-level novelty stimulus. Novelty activates dopaminergic circuits in the ventral tegmental area, producing the mild motivational reward associated with curiosity and pleasurable surprise (Fancourt & Finn, 2019). Over repeated visits, walkers may develop an anticipatory "what's there today?" orientation that subtly reframes the walk as a rewarding rather than merely functional activity.

**Prefrontal cortex & social cognition:** The act of reading a stranger's story fragment and imagining a response activates the prefrontal cortex and associated theory-of-mind networks, stimulating empathic inference and perspective-taking. This is the same neural substrate engaged in face-to-face social interaction, suggesting that asynchronous narrative exchange can produce genuine social cognition benefits in the absence of co-presence.

The cumulative effect of repeated novelty and social engagement may also support a shift toward parasympathetic nervous system dominance — a more contemplative, restorative state — though this is a hypothesised downstream effect rather than a directly measured outcome of this project.

## 1.4 The Palimpsest Effect: Ephemerality as Design Feature

The project's most distinctive theoretical contribution is the deliberate embracing of impermanence. Tasmanian rainfall functions not as a threat to the artwork but as its natural reset mechanism, ensuring the boardwalk surface is periodically cleared and made available for fresh contributions. This "palimpsest effect" — named for the manuscript practice of writing over partially erased text, where traces of earlier layers remain — creates a living, stratified record of community participation rather than a static installation.

This ephemerality directly addresses one of the most significant barriers to arts participation: aesthetic anxiety. Research in community arts consistently identifies fear of permanence and public judgement as deterrents to participation, particularly among adults and non-artists (National Centre for Creative Health, 2024). When participants know that their contribution will be washed away within days, the psychological stakes are reduced to near zero. The result is higher-frequency, lower-inhibition engagement from participants who would not contribute to a permanent installation.

This approach aligns with the Arts on Prescription model, in which low-barrier creative activity is deployed as a non-pharmaceutical intervention to build mental wellbeing and resilience. The boardwalk becomes, in effect, a self-renewing prescription: available to all, requiring no referral, and administered daily by the community itself.

## 1.5 Policy Alignment & Strategic Rationale

From a policy perspective, this project offers Huon Valley Council a low-cost, high-impact, and environmentally sustainable health promotion tool that utilises existing municipal infrastructure. No new structures are required; no permanent modifications to the boardwalk are proposed.

All chalk used in this project is standard classroom chalk supplied with a current Australian Safety Data Sheet (Educational Colours Pty Ltd, 2023), confirming no hazard classification under Safe Work Australia guidelines — not classified as hazardous to health, human health, or the environment. The chalk is not regulated as dangerous goods under ADG, IATA, or IMDG classifications. Standard SDS precautionary language advises against bulk disposal into waterways; in practice, the minute quantities deposited on the boardwalk surface by hand-drawing disperse below any measurable environmental threshold through normal rainfall, consistent with decades of chalk use in outdoor public spaces globally.

The project directly aligns with the National Centre for Creative Health (2024) framework, which identifies community-embedded creative health interventions as a priority mechanism for reducing pressure on primary healthcare services through upstream prevention. It also maps to the Huon Valley Health and Wellbeing Strategy's Action Area on Inclusion and Interaction, which calls for activating existing infrastructure to increase incidental social contact among isolated residents.

The project's built-in evaluation framework — using privacy-preserving Raspberry Pi Pico W-based IR sensors to measure baseline and intervention dwell times — provides Council with replicable, evidence-based data on the behavioural impact of creative placemaking interventions. This positions the Cygnet boardwalk pilot as a potential model for other Huon Valley sites and contributes to the emerging evidence base for community arts as public health infrastructure.

## 1.6 Learning from Previous Local Chalk Projects

Cygnet has a recent history of community chalk art initiatives, including a chalk haiku event organised during the COVID period that demonstrated both strong community appetite for this kind of activation and the importance of working carefully within Council's graffiti policy. That project navigated real constraints, and the Palimpsest Path is designed with those lessons in mind.

The key differences that distinguish this project from unsanctioned chalking or graffiti are:

- **Time-limited:** The project runs for a defined 8-week period, with a clear start and end date agreed with Council in advance.
- **Site-specific:** Activity is confined to the Lymington Road boardwalk only, making the scope easy to monitor and the project straightforward to conclude.
- **Documented materials:** All chalk is supplied with a current Australian Safety Data Sheet confirming non-hazardous, non-toxic classification. Material safety information is available on request.
- **Clearly branded:** All project chalk work carries a consistent visual identity and signage, making it immediately distinguishable from unrelated tagging or unsanctioned marking.
- **Built-in evaluation and exit:** The project includes a formal evaluation report at conclusion, and the site will be left in its original condition at project end.

The intention is to work with Council to create a carefully supervised pilot that channels community creative energy into one well-defined, health-promoting activity — and to generate evidence that could support future creative placemaking initiatives elsewhere in the Huon Valley.

## 1.7 The Causal Chain

The project's logic model can be stated as a single pathway:

```
+---------------------+   +-----------+   +-------------+   +------------------+   +----------------------+
|   Chalk stimulus    |-->|   Dwell   |-->|  Engagement |-->| Social cognition |-->| Wellbeing indicators |
|  (novelty/invite)   |   |           |   |             |   |                  |   |                      |
|                     |   | MEASURED  |   |  OBSERVED   |   |    INFERRED      |   |    ASSOCIATED        |
+---------------------+   +-----------+   +-------------+   +------------------+   +----------------------+
```

| Node | What it means | How it is established | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Chalk stimulus** | The visible intervention: dance steps, prompts, Happiness Index, narrative layers | Applied by project lead; documented photographically | Designed input |
| **Dwell** | A walker slows or stops in the gallery section; transit time exceeds the expected walking pace for 100m | Measured directly by Pico W IR sensors; threshold: >120s indicates deliberate pause | **Measured** |
| **Engagement** | A walker reads, contributes chalk, scans the QR code, or follows the dance steps | Inferred from dwell duration and photo-narrative contribution counts; QR scan data provides a partial direct signal | **Observed** (proxy) |
| **Social cognition** | Perspective-taking, empathic inference, imagined exchange with an unknown other | Inferred from engagement with narrative prompts; not directly measurable at this project scale | **Inferred** |
| **Wellbeing indicators** | Self-reported affect (Happiness Index); post-intervention persistence of elevated dwell suggesting continued value | Happiness Index provides a self-selected affective signal; persistence data is the project's strongest potential policy finding | **Associated** |

The chain is presented honestly: the first two links are measured; the third is observed through proxies; the fourth and fifth are inferred and associated respectively. This project does not claim to measure wellbeing directly — it claims to measure behaviour associated with the conditions that support wellbeing, and to observe self-reported affect from participants who choose to engage.
